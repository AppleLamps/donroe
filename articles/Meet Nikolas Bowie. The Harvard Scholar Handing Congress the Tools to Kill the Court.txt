Meet Nikolas Bowie. The Harvard Scholar Handing Congress the Tools to Kill the Court

In March 2025, Harvard Law Professor Nikolas Bowie sat down and wrote a short essay with a remarkable admission tucked inside it. Federal judges, he told his readers at the Law and Political Economy Project, “helped get us into this mess.” The mess in question is what he describes as lawless presidential behavior. The judges in question have spent, by his reckoning, a hundred and fifty years striking down laws passed by Congress. The cure he proposes is, in his own words, for the American people to stop relying on courts at all and to find power instead in strikes, rallies, art, and other institutions that do not require lawyers.

This is the capstone of one of the most disciplined academic campaigns of the last decade to delegitimize the United States Supreme Court. And anyone who thinks the courts are the last functional guardrail against political overreach should be paying close attention to where it is heading.

The Credentials Are the Disguise
Bowie is not a marginal figure. He clerked for Justice Sonia Sotomayor after graduating from Harvard Law. He holds a PhD in history from Harvard. He is the Louis D. Brandeis Professor of Law at Harvard Law School, one of the most senior chairs in American legal academia. He sits on the boards of the ACLU of Massachusetts, Lawyers for Civil Rights, and the People’s Parity Project, a progressive organization devoted to court reform. His work has appeared in the Harvard Law Review, the Yale Law Journal, the Stanford Law Review, the Virginia Law Review, the New York Times, the Atlantic, and the Washington Post.

Those credentials matter because they make the argument he has been building since 2018 sound reasonable. When a random activist says the Supreme Court is illegitimate, the country shrugs. When a Harvard Law professor with a Sotomayor clerkship and a Yale Law Journal byline says the same thing in slightly more elegant prose, senators start drafting bills.

That is exactly what has happened.

The Thesis, in Plain English
Strip the footnotes away and Bowie’s argument is straightforward. Judicial review, the Court’s power to strike down laws as unconstitutional, is not in the Constitution. The Court invented it in Marbury v. Madison in 1803 and then largely left it alone for half a century. The real machinery of what he calls “judicial supremacy” was built after the Civil War, when the Court shut down Reconstruction, invalidated civil rights laws, and taught itself to override Congress. Since then, on his account, the Court has been a rider sitting on top of Congress, usually giving the horse free rein but always ready to yank the reins when Congress tries to do anything that threatens hierarchies of wealth, race, or status.

Therefore, Bowie argues, the country should stop treating the Court as the final voice on what the Constitution means. Congress, elected by the people, should be able to pass legislation asserting its own reading of the Constitution. The Court, staffed by unelected lawyers with lifetime tenure, should be demoted to something closer to an umpire in state-versus-federal disputes.

The book-length version of this argument, Supremacy: How Rule by the Court Replaced Government by the People, co-authored with his Harvard colleague Daphna Renan, is forthcoming from W. W. Norton in 2026. Endorsements are already pouring in from Jill Lepore, Annette Gordon-Reed, and Sherrilyn Ifill. The academic infrastructure for this move is in place.

The Seven-Year March
Reading his published work in reverse is a tour through a carefully escalating argument.

2018. Bowie arrives at Harvard as an assistant professor and uses his inaugural pieces to attack the Court’s emerging First Amendment jurisprudence. In the Take Care blog, his essay on NIFLA v. Becerra and Janus v. AFSCME frames the Roberts Court as weaponizing free speech for deregulatory ends. His Harvard Law Review Forum piece High Crimes Without Law sets up a theory of impeachment that sidelines the Court.

2019. The Government-Could-Not-Work Doctrine in the Virginia Law Review takes direct aim at Janus and the doctrine that compulsory laws trigger strict scrutiny. The argument is clever and surgical. If accepted, it strips the Court of one of its most important tools for policing public-sector labor regulation. The same year, his Harvard Law Review book review Corporate Personhood v. Corporate Statehood uses Citizens United as a springboard to argue that corporate political speech is structurally antidemocratic.

2021. Three interlocking pieces land in a single year. Antidemocracy in the Harvard Law Review uses the Cedar Point Nursery takings case as a vehicle for a full-throated attack on the Court as a structural protector of property over popular majorities. Well over a hundred fifty scholarly citations later, it has become a foundational text of the court-disempowerment movement. The Washington Post op-ed How the Supreme Court Dominates Our Democracy translates the academic case for a broader audience. The subtitle is unambiguous about where he is going… Judicial review gives any five justices power over the whole government, and he wants readers to ask why.

Then on June 30, 2021, he testifies before President Biden’s Presidential Commission on the Supreme Court. This is the document to read if you want to understand the endgame. He calls the Court an antidemocratic institution. He calls judicial review an antidemocratic superweapon. He tells the commissioners, in writing, that the Court’s relationship to Congress is not that of an umpire overseeing a batter but of a rider overseeing a horse. He urges the United States to aspire to be as democratic as New Zealand, a country whose Parliament can simply override its courts. He urges the commission to evaluate every reform proposal with an eye toward disempowering the Court.

2022. The Separation-of-Powers Counterrevolution appears in the Yale Law Journal, co-written with Renan. This is the scholarly foundation stone. The argument is that the modern Supreme Court’s separation-of-powers doctrine, beginning with Myers v. United States in 1926, was not a neutral legal development. It was, in their telling, a Lost Cause reaction by post-Reconstruction intellectuals and a future Chief Justice named William Howard Taft, designed to prevent Congress from ever again empowering a Reconstruction-style assault on white Southern power. The piece went on to win the American Bar Association Administrative Law and Regulatory Practice Section’s 2023 Annual Scholarship Award, the most prestigious administrative law prize in the country, and now sits at nearly two hundred citations in legal scholarship. In June of the same year, the Atlantic op-ed The Supreme Court Is Not Supposed to Have This Much Power delivers the argument to the general reader. The subtitle is the legislative program in six words: And Congress should claw it back.

2024. In October, with Renan again in the New York Times, Bowie shifts from diagnosis to prescription. The Supreme Court Has Grown Too Powerful. Congress Must Intervene. endorses the No Kings Act, a bill introduced by Senate Majority Leader Chuck Schumer in response to the Trump v. United States immunity ruling. The No Kings Act would strip the Supreme Court of jurisdiction to decide whether the Act is constitutional, leave enforcement to lower federal courts, and require those courts to presume the Act is constitutional. Bowie and Renan frame this not as an aggressive norm-break but as an old and admirable American tradition.

2025. The LPE essay in March marks the final shift. Having spent seven years arguing that the Court is illegitimate, Bowie now tells readers that the judiciary cannot be relied on to save democracy from a lawless executive. The argument has closed its own loop. The Court is too weak to save you. The Court is the reason you need saving. The only real power is political and social, outside the courts entirely.

From Ivory Tower to Senate Floor
None of this would matter if it stayed in law reviews. It has not.

On August 1, 2024, Senate Majority Leader Chuck Schumer introduced S. 4973, titled the No Kings Act, alongside thirty-four Democratic and independent co-sponsors. The bill is a direct operationalization of the Bowie-Renan framework. It declares, by simple statute, that presidents are not immune from federal criminal laws. It then invokes the Exceptions Clause of Article III to strip the Supreme Court of appellate jurisdiction over challenges to the Act. It requires lower federal courts to presume the Act is constitutional. It leaves the Court legally gagged from reviewing whether Congress had the power to do this in the first place. The co-sponsor roster reads like a who’s who of progressive leadership. Warren. Sanders. Durbin. Booker. Gillibrand. Merkley. Warnock. Whitehouse. Markey. This is not a backbencher stunt. It is the Senate Democratic leadership, in writing, endorsing the exact mechanism Bowie has spent seven years building the intellectual case for.

And the No Kings Act is only one front.

In April 2021, Senator Ed Markey and Representatives Jerrold Nadler, Hank Johnson, and Mondaire Jones introduced the Judiciary Act of 2021, which would expand the Supreme Court from nine justices to thirteen by simple statute. The bill eventually drew close to fifty congressional co-sponsors and secured the official endorsement of the Congressional Progressive Caucus under Chairwoman Pramila Jayapal. The same coalition reintroduced the bill in 2023 with additional sponsors. A third track pushes for eighteen-year term limits on sitting justices, imposed by statute rather than by constitutional amendment, in direct tension with Article III’s guarantee that federal judges hold their offices during good behavior.

Each of these proposals operates on a different mechanism. Jurisdiction-stripping. Court-packing. Statutory term limits. Taken together they form a multi-front legislative campaign against the structural integrity of the judiciary. The intellectual case for all three has been laid down, peer-reviewed, and cited in elite legal scholarship, largely by a single Harvard Law professor and his collaborator. Bowie is on the board of the People’s Parity Project, whose entire organizational purpose is exactly this work. He co-leads reading groups for the LPE Project alongside Samuel Moyn, Aziz Rana, Ryan Doerfler, Amy Kapczynski, and Astra Taylor, a roll call of the academic front organized around court reform.

This is a movement with a professor, a book, model statutes, Senate sponsors, and an activist network. It is patient and it is credentialed and it is already years into execution.

The Sleight of Hand
The headline claim of this movement… that judicial review is not in the Constitution, that the Court stole it in 1803, that the Founders never intended it… is where the scholarly gloss starts to wear thin.

Article III vests the judicial power in one Supreme Court. Article VI, the Supremacy Clause, makes the Constitution the supreme law of the land binding judges in every state. Alexander Hamilton’s Federalist No. 78 spelled out, before ratification, that courts would be required to declare acts contrary to the Constitution void. The same ratification conventions that approved the Constitution debated judicial review openly. State courts had been exercising it against state statutes for years before Marbury. The claim that Chief Justice Marshall invented the power out of thin air in 1803 is a tidy story that survives only by ignoring two decades of pre-Marshall state court practice and the explicit text of the Founders’ own advocacy.

The second move is the framing of judicial review as inherently antidemocratic. It is true that unelected judges striking down statutes is, narrowly defined, a countermajoritarian act. It is also the point. The Bill of Rights does not ask majorities for permission. The First Amendment protects unpopular speech from majoritarian censorship. The Second Amendment protects gun rights from majoritarian confiscation. The Fourth Amendment protects the unpopular criminal defendant from majoritarian law enforcement. Every single one of those protections requires an institution willing to tell a majority of Congress, and a majority of voters, no.

Bowie’s framework quietly calls all of that antidemocratic. His preferred alternative, what he and Renan call political constitutionalism, means that the people with legislative power get to say what the Constitution means. The party in power gets the Constitution it wants. That is not democracy in any meaningful sense…

The Argument That Collapses Itself

The deepest contradiction in Bowie’s project is one he published in his own name. In The Imaginary Immigration Clause, his 2022 Michigan Law Review article co-written with Norah Rast, Bowie argues that the Supreme Court wrongly defers to Congress on immigration. The reason courts should not defer, he writes, is that Congress has a long historical record of being hostile and overzealous toward disenfranchised minorities, and relying on the Court to protect those minorities has proved to be a flawed strategy anyway.

Take that claim at face value. A co-equal branch of Congress, on his own telling, cannot be trusted to respect the rights of politically disfavored groups. He uses this as an argument that courts should not have deferred to Congress’s immigration judgments.

Then read his other work. The entire edifice of Antidemocracy, The Separation-of-Powers Counterrevolution, the SCOTUS Commission testimony, the Atlantic op-ed, and the forthcoming book demands that Americans hand unreviewable final authority over constitutional meaning to the exact same Congress he elsewhere describes as hostile and overzealous toward minorities. The institution whose record on civil rights is, in his own telling, so compromised that it cannot be trusted to write immigration statutes is the same institution he proposes should have the final word on the First Amendment, the Second Amendment, the Fourth Amendment, religious liberty, property rights, and free speech.

Either Congress is a trustworthy constitutional interpreter and the courts should defer on immigration… or Congress is hostile and overzealous and a judicial check on it is indispensable. Bowie wants to have both at once. He wants judicial skepticism of Congress when it helps progressive outcomes, and legislative supremacy over the judiciary when it helps progressive outcomes. What is on offer is outcome selection dressed up as historiography.

The One-Way Ratchet
If any of this were being argued neutrally, you would expect to see it applied evenly across the Court’s history. You do not. The Bowie-Renan book argues that even the celebrated rulings of the Warren Court era, including the civil rights decisions the American left treats as sacred, were less important than commonly believed and in some ways counterproductive. That is a staggering move. It removes the one set of rulings that might complicate the argument, by reframing them as historical accidents that delayed rather than enabled racial progress.

What gets left, once that move is made, is a Supreme Court that is bad when conservatives win and not all that good when progressives win. The practical effect of the argument is always the same direction of force… toward weakening the Court whenever it stands in the way of congressional majorities. Those congressional majorities will, of course, sometimes be Republican. But the institutional reforms Bowie endorses, jurisdiction-stripping, statutory override of constitutional rulings, political defiance of judicial orders, are not symmetrical in effect. They are a one-way ratchet. Once the norm is established that Congress can simply declare the Court wrong and strip its jurisdiction over whatever bill it wants, there is no going back to the previous equilibrium.

Imagine the Bowie framework applied by a future Republican Congress to, say, a Supreme Court ruling striking down a national abortion ban, or a ruling protecting sanctuary cities, or a ruling that extends Obergefell. The same jurisdiction-stripping logic that Schumer deploys against Trump v. United States becomes a template for stripping the Court’s jurisdiction over any ruling the majority finds inconvenient. Progressive legal scholars who have cheered this project are, in effect, writing the instruction manual for a future political majority that does not share their values.

What He Actually Wants
Bowie is not subtle about the endgame when he speaks to friendly audiences. At an LPE event previewing the forthcoming book, Renan said their goal is federal authority without judicial supremacy, which she defined as rejecting the Court’s final say on what the Constitution means. The model they explicitly cite is mid-nineteenth-century political abolitionism, in which figures like Frederick Douglass and Gerrit Smith refused to treat the Dred Scott decision as the final word and instead pushed Congress and the people to override it through political action.

Bowie makes the parallel more specific in interviews about the forthcoming book. His model for the proper relationship between Congress and the Court is the Reconstruction Congress that passed the Civil Rights Act of 1866, legislatively defining national citizenship in direct contradiction to the Supreme Court’s holding in Dred Scott and then, in his phrasing, protecting that legislation from what he calls judicial sabotage. That is his blueprint. When the Court rules in a way Congress disagrees with, Congress should legislate directly against the ruling and structurally shield the legislation from review.

This is, to be clear, a noble historical tradition when applied to Dred Scott. It was also the prelude to a civil war. The fact that Bowie’s intellectual lineage ends at a moment when Americans were willing to kill each other over whose reading of the Constitution would prevail is worth sitting with. When he writes, in March 2025, that Trump’s defiance of federal law is a fun-house mirror of the Court’s own defiance of Congress, he is drawing the structural equivalence on purpose. The message is that “lawless presidents” are a symmetrical response to a “lawless Court”, and the solution is neither the president nor the Court but popular mobilization outside both.

This is how you get from a tenured chair at Harvard Law to an argument that organized political action should take priority over judicial orders.

Implications for the American Constitutional Order
Nikolas Bowie’s scholarship is formidable. He is an eloquent writer, a meticulous historian, and a highly persuasive advocate. Through his positions at Harvard Law School, his prominent board memberships, and his frequent appearances in elite media outlets, he is shaping the minds of the next generation of American lawyers and judges.

However, his project is deeply alarming. Over the past several years, progressive politicians have grown increasingly hostile to the Supreme Court simply because they no longer control its personnel. Rather than attempting to win elections and appoint judges who share their interpretative philosophy, the academic Left, led by scholars like Nikolas Bowie, has decided to burn the institution to the ground.

By systematically attacking judicial review as “antidemocratic,” attempting to shatter the unitary executive, advocating for jurisdiction stripping, and promoting the myth of legislative supremacy, Bowie is laying the intricate legal and intellectual groundwork to dismantle the United States Supreme Court.

If his theories transition from the pages of the Harvard Law Review into the halls of Congress... as they are already beginning to do... the United States will cease to be a constitutional republic governed by the rule of law. It will become a majoritarian state where fundamental liberties... from free speech and religious exercise to the right to bear arms and own property... exist only at the sufferance of the political party currently holding power in Washington. The conservative defense of the Supreme Court is a defense of the Constitution itself against the very real, very present threat of academic dismantlement.
