The coverage
Now let me show you the war you’ve been told about.

From the Wall Street Journal, March 14, citing unnamed US officials: five KC-135 refueling aircraft damaged by an Iranian strike on a Saudi air base. Trump responded the next day by naming the WSJ directly on Truth Social. His exact words: the five US refueling planes that were supposedly struck down and badly damaged according to the Wall Street Journal’s false reporting are all in service, with the exception of one, which will soon be flying the skies.

That is a sitting president publicly accusing a flagship American newspaper of running Iranian disinformation, citing specific data points, offering a factual correction. The WSJ did not substantially retract or amend. The “US losing aircraft” narrative continued propagating through other outlets as established fact. The story became a template.

From CBS News coverage of the Minab school strike, March 9: Iranian state media and health officials have reported that the strike killed dozens of people, including schoolgirls ages 7 to 12. Iranian officials have blamed the US and Israel for the strike.

Two paragraphs later in the same piece: a person familiar with the ongoing inquiry told CBS News that US investigators believe the US may have been responsible. The Iranian regime’s attribution was on the first reference. By the second reference, the framing had already shifted. By the time this reached the Senate floor, the Iranian origin of the claim had been erased entirely.

From Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer’s formal floor remarks, March 11, official Congressional record: “Thousands of people in the Middle East have been killed, including 170 at an all-girls elementary school that initial reports suggested may have been the result of US strikes.”

Schumer stated a specific casualty figure of 170 as fact on the Senate floor. The figure came from the Iranian regime. It cannot be independently verified. The Western human rights organizations that have reported on the strike have explicitly acknowledged they could not speak to witnesses due to the regime’s imposed internet blackout. Human Rights Watch stated directly they were unable to safely speak with witnesses. Amnesty International stated directly they were unable to speak directly with witnesses and victims’ families due to the deliberate internet shutdown imposed by Iranian authorities.

Schumer cited the number anyway. As a fact. In formal remarks. Official Senate record.

From Senator Tammy Duckworth’s war powers resolution press release, April 15, co-signed by eleven other Democratic senators including Murphy, Booker, Kaine, Schiff, Baldwin, Merkley, Gillibrand, Van Hollen, Kelly, Warnock, and Kim: Trump’s “unjustified war of choice” at 46 days, with Duckworth stating in her direct quote that it is “completely and utterly outrageous that Senate Republicans are once again ceding their Constitutional authority to this warmongering, wanna-be dictator who is hellbent on throwing our servicemembers into needless danger.”

“Warmongering, wanna-be dictator.” The sitting Senate Minority Whip, formal Senate letterhead, describing a sitting US commander-in-chief actively commanding forces in combat operations.

Senator Van Hollen’s quote in the same release: Trump’s actions have “killed thousands of civilians across the Middle East.”

Thousands of civilians. No independent sourcing. Iranian regime numbers, cited as established fact, on official Senate Democratic letterhead, in a formal press release.

The laundering pipeline
Let me show you the mechanism that connects the regime’s state media desk to the Senate floor speech. It has six stages and every one is documented.

Stage one. The Iranian regime produces casualty figures through state channels. In the Minab school case, Foreign Ministry spokesman Baghaei said 150 on February 28. IRNA and the city prosecutor said 165 by March 1. The Ministry of Education said 156 civilians including 120 children. The regime controls the site, the communications, and the body count. No Western journalist has been permitted to verify anything independently. The regime has imposed a nationwide internet blackout explicitly to control the narrative.

Stage two. Diaspora groups relay information from contacts inside Iran through whatever channels remain open during the shutdown. A Canada-based teachers’ union representative. The Norway-based Hengaw rights group. None of these sources is at the scene. They are passing along what they are told by people they know, during a regime-imposed information blackout.

Stage three. Western human rights organizations produce hedged reports. Amnesty International uses careful language, “reportedly killed,” “Iranian authorities say.” Human Rights Watch does the same. The hedging is appropriate. The sourcing is secondhand. These organizations are being honest about what they can and cannot verify.

Stage four. Major media outlets cite the human rights organizations. The hedging language begins to drop. “Iranian authorities say 168 killed” becomes “168 were killed” becomes “the deadliest civilian strike of the war.” CBS does this relatively gradually with some attribution preserved. The WSJ does it more aggressively. The NYT uses framing language like “crossed a new Rubicon” that treats the underlying regime claims as established premise.

Stage five. Political actors cite the washed-through numbers as established fact. Schumer says 170 on the Senate floor. Duckworth’s press release says “thousands of civilians killed.” Van Hollen repeats “thousands of civilians.” The language is coordinated across senators, which means a caucus whip message is operating, which means the Iranian regime’s casualty narrative is now embedded in formal Democratic Senate messaging infrastructure.

Stage six. Progressive outlets amplify and inflate. Jacobin publishes “bombs killing Iranian children.” The Nation uses “at least 180 people incinerated at a primary school in Minab, most of them young girls.”

Watch what happens to the number across the chain. Iranian regime sources range from 150 to 168. Schumer uses 170. The Nation uses 180. The number grew in the laundering. This is a classic propaganda signature. When a claim serves a political purpose, it inflates as it moves through partisan channels. The underlying claim was already unverifiable. The derivative claims are further divorced from the source.
